Short essay · on desire

Feeling anger is human.
It just doesn't move the curve.

Frustration, irritation, even moments of resentment when the other person's desire doesn't match yours — these are normal emotions. You're not a monster for having felt them. The point is something else: these emotions, however understandable, don't change the outcome. What changes the outcome is only how you behave after feeling them. But before talking about emotions, we need to understand what we're actually looking at.

I.

The two curves, first of all.

Sexual desire doesn't work the same way in everyone. The average difference between men and women isn't "one always wants it, the other is moody." It's something else, more precise: two different ignition systems.

man · spontaneous desire woman · responsive desire

Both curves move. But they move differently, and for different reasons. Notice the red peaks: when the context is right, the female curve can rise higher than the male one.

Male nature · on average

Spontaneous desire

Starts on its own. It arrives before the context — a thought, an image, an idea — and then looks for the occasion. More constant, more independent of environment, but still influenced by stress, sleep, health, age. The flat line up top is a myth: this curve oscillates too, simply with smaller amplitude.

Female nature · on average

Responsive desire

Responds. It needs something first — safety, attention, context, connection, the right moment. It's not weaker or more "complicated": it has a different ignition. When the context is right, it can rise higher than the male curve. When it's missing, it drops. Not out of caprice: because of how it's built.

This isn't "she's strange and I'm normal." It's: two different engines. One starts from rest, the other starts when you turn the key. A car that needs a key isn't inferior to one with a push button. It's built differently. And knowing this changes everything, because it stops looking like a defect and starts looking like an instruction manual.

II.

Three practical things the curve teaches you.

Once you understand the shape of the curve, some things that previously seemed contradictory stop being so. Three in particular confuse almost every man, because they have no direct experience of how a responsive system works.

01

When the moment is there, it is the moment.

If tonight you're connected, she's present, there's play and attention and the wave is high — that is the moment. Don't postpone it to tomorrow because you're tired, because there's a series to finish, because you think you have "all the time in the world." Responsive desire doesn't work like spontaneous desire: tomorrow the same external situation can produce a completely different internal temperature, because in the meantime sleep, stress, context, mood, and conversations have all shifted.

This isn't "take advantage before she changes her mind" — it's the opposite: it's responding to presence with presence. When she's really there, be there too. It's respect for the shared moment, not exploitation of a window. The difference is one you feel inside: one is selfishness, the other is attention.

02

After the high wave, the low wave almost always follows.

One evening you're connected, there's intensity, she's right there with you. The next day she doesn't reply to your messages for hours, she's distant, she seems like a different person. Almost every man, faced with this, short-circuits: if yesterday she was like that, why is she like this today? And that short-circuit produces frustration, frustration produces pressure, pressure lowers the curve even further. The damage is self-inflicted.

The truth, without sugaring it: the curve drops. That's it. Sometimes it comes back up, sometimes lower, sometimes not at all and the interest really does fade. None of these three possibilities depend on your hours of anxiety the next day — in fact, anxiety transmitted via messages is precisely one of the things that worsens the odds of it coming back. At the start of a story there's no established bond that "holds the line"; there's only the temperature of the moment, and that oscillates.

The right move isn't to chase, isn't to question why, isn't to send three messages when one would do. It's continue living your life and let the wave do what it does. If it rises, great. If it doesn't, you've learned something about real compatibility before investing months. Either way, you've won by avoiding the move that would have made you lose anyway.

03

Intimacy in the moment is the most powerful force you have.

If there's one thing worth saying clearly: sex done right, in the right moment, is one of the most powerful tools of connection that exist between two people at the start of a story. Not in an abstract way — in a physical, immediate way, measurable in the temperature of the relationship in the days that follow. Oxytocin, shared vulnerability, having crossed something intense together: all of this changes the weight that person carries in the other's life.

This is why postponing a moment of real intimacy at the start of a story — out of laziness, out of pride, out of "maybe tomorrow," out of testing something — often costs you the story itself. The intensity of a shared moment isn't infinitely renewable: if that night it was there and you didn't live it, tomorrow isn't the same night, it's a different night, with different internal data, and that connection might not return to the same level.

Living the moment when the moment is there — this is almost always the right move. Not as a tactic to get something from her, but as a choice to be there when being there matters. The difference is one you feel inside, and over time she feels it too.

III.

Emotions are data, not instructions.

When you feel frustration, your brain is telling you: something I wanted didn't arrive. That's useful information. The problem starts when you turn the information into a general verdict — when you move from "I'm frustrated with this situation" to "women are broken," "they don't make sense," "they're all like that." That's the move that does the real damage.

The damage isn't inside a single interaction. It's that contempt, once it forms as a general attitude toward women, follows you everywhere. It's heard in the tone of your messages before it's even spoken. It's seen in your gaze, in how you carry yourself when you enter a room, in how you listen — or don't listen. Women perceive it long before they can name it, and they close up before you've even understood why. Result: every new story starts with a brake already on, and you confirm to yourself that "they're all the same." It's a self-fulfilling prophecy, built one frustration at a time.

So: feel the anger, recognize it, but don't let it generalize. A situation that didn't go the way you wanted is a data point about that moment, not about half the human race. Anger is a passenger, not the driver — and especially not the cartographer who redraws the map of the world after every curve you don't like.

IV.

The outcome doesn't change because you're angry.

This is the hard part to accept. If her curve is responsive — more sensitive to context, stress, sleep, mood, how you've behaved over the last 48 hours — no amount of frustration on your part will realign it to yours. Zero. Worse: the more pressure grows, the more the curve drops, because responsive desire needs safety to emerge. Pressure and responsive desire are physically incompatible.

So getting angry isn't just useless — it's counterproductive. It's literally the thing that produces the least of what you want.

Frustration is allowed. Frustration in the driver's seat is the end of everything.
V.

The real frame — three words.

One

Patience

The curves meet over time, not on command. Patience doesn't mean resignation: it means accepting the other person's rhythm as a fact, not as an obstacle to break through.

Two

Acceptance

She isn't a defective version of you. She works differently. Accepting this is the minimum condition for anything good to happen. Without acceptance, every gesture of hers toward you sounds like a concession, not like desire.

Three

Push

Don't push her. Push yourself. Keep creating context, attention, presence, play. Responsive desire responds to something — and that something, in large part, you build, with consistency and without keeping a tab.

VI.

Honest summary.

Feeling anger: allowed.
Showing contempt: destroys the thing you want.
Waiting passively: doesn't work.
Patience + acceptance + constant work: the only strategy that produces what you're looking for.

This isn't a romantic frame. It's a practical one. It's the only one that takes you where you want to go, because all the others — pressure, sulking, resentment, keeping score — take you in the opposite direction. You don't need to forbid yourself emotions. You need to notice them, and then choose the right move anyway.
